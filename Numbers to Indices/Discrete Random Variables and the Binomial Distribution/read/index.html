Example

Given that a year is 365 days and each day has the same probability of being someone's birthday. What's the probability of at least 2 people sharing a birthday in a group of 25 people? Now, calculating each of the possible outcomes could become very tedious. That is calculating the odds that 2 people share a birthday, 3 people, 4 people, etc. So instead we try to find out the odds that no one in the group shares a birthday and subtract those odds from 1 (100%).

First, let's look at the odds of only two people having distinct birthdays.

365365⋅364365=0.9973\displaystyle\frac{365}{365} \cdot \displaystyle\frac{364}{365} = 0.9973365365​⋅365364​=0.9973

Person one can be born on any day and the odds of having a distinct birthday are therefore 1. The next person can be born on everyday but the 1 the other person was born on, so 364 days

Now let's say we add the 3rd person and calculate his/her odds of having a distinct birthday.

365365⋅364365⋅363365=0.9918\displaystyle\frac{365}{365} \cdot \displaystyle\frac{364}{365} \cdot \displaystyle\frac{363}{365} = 0.9918365365​⋅365364​⋅365363​=0.9918

This can also be rewritten as

365⋅364⋅3633653\displaystyle\frac{365 \cdot 364 \cdot 363}{365^3}3653365⋅364⋅363​

And we can do this on and on for all the 25 people we are interested in. But that may also become a bit tedious. So we use factorials instead. So instead of doing

365⋅364⋅363⋯⋅34136525\displaystyle\frac{365 \cdot 364 \cdot 363 \dots \cdot 341}{365^{25}}36525365⋅364⋅363⋯⋅341​

we do

Essentially the division of factorials here removes all the values that are less than 341341341, making the two expressions completely equal. Rewriting the expression like this is only done to simplify the task of typing it up for the computer to calculate, since most calculators don't understand …\dots….

or roughly 57% odds of at least 2 people in a group of 25 sharing the same birthday.

This example is commonly known as the birthday paradox, since it seems to many people to be counter-intuitive that the probability of at least two people out of 25 sharing a birthday is higher than 50% when there are 365 birthdays to choose from.

Simple Surveys With Replacement​

If we randomly draw individuals (with replacement), and ask a question with two possible answers (positive or negative), then the number of positive answers will come from a binomial distribution.

Examples​

Example

Suppose we are participating in a lottery. We pick a number from a lottery bowl (a simple random sample). We can put the number aside, or we can put it back into the bowl. If we put the number back in the bowl, it may be selected more than once; if we put it aside, it can be selected only one time.

Definition: When an element can be selected more than one time, we are sampling with replacement.

Definition: When an element can be selected only one time, we are sampling without replacement.

The Binomial Distribution​

If we toss a biased coin nnn independent times, each with probability ppp of landing heads up, then the probability of obtaining xxx heads is

(nx)px(1−p)n−x\displaystyle{n \choose x}p^x (1-p)^{n-x}(xn​)px(1−p)n−x

Examples​

Example

Suppose we toss a coin, with probability ppp of landing on heads nnn times ,obtaining a sequence of H's (when it lands heads) and T's (when it lands tails). Any sequence

HTH…HTHHHHTH \dots HTHHHHTH…HTHHH

which has xxx heads (HHH) and n−xn-xn−x tails (TTT), has the probability px(1−p)n−xp^x(1-p)^{n-x}px(1−p)n−x. There are exactly (nx)\displaystyle\binom{n}{x}(xn​) such sequences, so the total probability of landing xxx heads in nnn tosses is

(nx)px(1−p)n−x\displaystyle\binom{n}{x}p^x(1-p)^{n-x}(xn​)px(1−p)n−x

Suppose a youngster puts his shirt on by himself every day for five days. The probability that he puts it on the right way each time is p=0.2p=0.2p=0.2. We let XXX be a random variable that describes the number of times the youngster puts his shirt on the right way. We can now calculate for example the probability that the youngster will put it on the right way for at least four days

This is possible to calculate in R in a several ways, either using the command dbinom or dbinom.

The command dbinom calculates

P(X=k)P(X = k)P(X=k)

and the command pbinom calculates

P(X≤k)P(X \leq k)P(X≤k)

where kkk is the number of successful trials.

The fourth way of calculating this in R is to use pbinom. As said before pbinom calculates

P(X≤k)P(X \leq k)P(X≤k)

The Expected Value or Population Mean​

The expected value is the sum of the possible outcomes, weighted with the respective probabilities (discrete variable). Think of this in terms of an urn full of marbles, each labelled with number.

Details​

If the possible outcomes are x1,x2,…x_1, x_2, \dotsx1​,x2​,… with probabilities p1,p2,…p_1, p_2, \dotsp1​,p2​,… then the expected value is:

μ=x1⋅p1+x2⋅p2+…\mu=x_1 \cdot p_1+x_2 \cdot p_2 + \ldotsμ=x1​⋅p1​+x2​⋅p2​+…

The fact that this is the only sensible definition of an expected value follows from considering random draws from a finite population where there are nin_ini​ possibilities of obtaining the value xix_ixi​. If we set n=∑xin=\sum x_in=∑xi​ and pi=ni/np_i=n_i/npi​=ni​/n then the expected value above is the simple average of all the numbers in the original population.

In the case of the binomial distribution with nnn trials and success probability ppp it turns out that:

μ=n⋅p\mu=n \cdot pμ=n⋅p

If XXX is the corresponding random variable, we denote this quantity by E[X]E[X]E[X].

The Population Variance​

The (population) variance, for a discrete distribution, is:

σ2=E[(X−μ)2]=(x1−μ)2p1+(x2−μ)2p2+…\sigma^2 = E\left[ \left ( X-\mu \right ) ^2 \right ] = (x_1 - \mu)^2 p_1 + (x_2 - \mu)^2 p_2 + \dotsσ2=E[(X−μ)2]=(x1​−μ)2p1​+(x2​−μ)2p2​+…

where it is understood that the random variable XXX has this distribution and μ\muμ is the expected value.

In the case of the binomial distribution, it turns out that:

σ2=np(1−p)\sigma^2 = np(1 - p)σ2=np(1−p)

Details​

Definition

If μ\muμ is the expected value, then the variance of a discrete distribution is defined as

σ2=(x1−μ)2p1+(x2−μ)2p2+…\sigma ^2=(x_1 - \mu)^2 p_1 + (x_2 - \mu)^2 p_2 + \ldotsσ2=(x1​−μ)2p1​+(x2​−μ)2p2​+…

σ2=Var[X]=E[(X−μ)2]\sigma^2 = Var[X]=E\left [ \left ( X - \mu \right ) ^ 2\right ]σ2=Var[X]=E[(X−μ)2]
