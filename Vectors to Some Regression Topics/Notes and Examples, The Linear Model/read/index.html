Notes and Examples: The Linear Model

Simple Linear Regression In R​

To test the effect of one variable on another, simple linear regression may be applied. The fitted model may be expressed as y=α+β^xy=\alpha + \hat{\beta} xy=α+β^​x, where α\alphaα is a constant, β^\hat{\beta}β^​ is the estimated coefficient, and xxx is the explanatory variable.

Figure: Example taken from R of a fitted model using linear regression.

Details​

Below is the linear regression output using the R's data set car. Notice that the output from the model may be divided into two main categories:

  1. output that assesses the model as a whole, and

  2. output that relates to the estimated coefficients for the model

Call: lm(formula = dist ~ speed, data = cars)

Residuals: Min 1Q Median 3Q Max -29.069 -9.525 -2.272 9.215 43.201

Coefficients: Estimate Std.
Error t value Pr(>|t|) (Intercept) -17.5791 6.7584 -2.601 0.0123 * speed 3.9324 0.4155 9.464 1.49e-12 *** ---

Residual standard error: 15.38 on 48 degrees of freedom Multiple R-squared: 0.6511, Adjusted R-squared: 0.6438 F-statistic: 89.57 on 1 and 48 DF, p-value: 1.490e-12

Notice that there are four different sets of output (Call, Residuals, Coefficients, and Results) for both the constant α\alphaα and the estimated coefficient β^\hat{\beta}β^​ speed variable.

The estimated coefficients describe the change in the dependent variable when there is a single unit increase in the explanatory variable given that everything else is held constant.

The standard error is a measure of accuracy and is used to construct the confidence interval. Confidence intervals provide a range of values for which there is a set level of confidence that the true population mean will be within the given range. For example, if the CI is set at 95% percent then the probability of observing a value outside the given CI range is less than 0.05

The ppp-value is represented as a percentage. Specifically, the ppp-value indicates the percentage of time, given that your null hypothesis is true, that you would find an outcome at least as extreme as the observed value. If your calculated ppp-value is 0.020.020.02 then 2% of the time you'd observe a mean at least as large as your observed

In the overall model assessment the R-squared is the explained variance over the total variance. Generally, a higher R2R^2R2 is better but data with very little variance makes it easy to achieve a higher R2R^2R2, which is why the adjusted R2R^2R2 is presented

Lastly, the FFF-statistic is given. Since the ttt-Statistic is not appropriate to compare two or more coefficients, the FFF-statistic must be applied. The basic methodology is that it compares a restricted model where the coefficients have been set to a certain fixed level to a model which is unrestricted. The most common is the sum of squared residuals FFF-test.

Multiple Linear Regression​

The One-way Model​

The one-way ANOVA model is of the form:

Yij=μi+ϵijY_{ij}=\mu_i+\epsilon_{ij}Yij​=μi​+ϵij​

or

Yij=μ+αi+ϵijY_{ij}=\mu+\alpha_i+\epsilon_{ij}Yij​=μ+αi​+ϵij​

Details​

The one-way ANOVA model is of the form:

Yij=μi+ϵijY_{ij}=\mu_i+\epsilon_{ij}Yij​=μi​+ϵij​

where YijY_{ij}Yij​ is observation jjj in treatment group iii and μi\mu_iμi​ are the parameters of the model and are means of treatment group iii. The ϵij\epsilon_{ij}ϵij​ are independent and follow a normal distribution with mean zero and constant variance σ2\sigma^2σ2 often written as ϵ∼N(0,σ2)\epsilon\sim N(0,\sigma^2)ϵ∼N(0,σ2).

The ANOVA model can also be written in the form:

Yij=μ+αi+ϵijY_{ij}=\mu+\alpha_i+\epsilon_{ij}Yij​=μ+αi​+ϵij​

where μ\muμ is the overall mean of all treatment groups and αi\alpha_iαi​ is the deviation of mean of treatment group iii from the overall mean. The ϵij\epsilon_{ij}ϵij​ follow a normal distribution as before

The expected value of YijY_{ij}Yij​ is μi\mu_iμi​ as the expected value of the errors is zero, often written as E[Yij]=μiE[Y_{ij}]=\mu_iE[Yij​]=μi​.

Examples​

Example

In the rat diet experiment the model would be of the form:

yij=μi+ϵijy_{ij}=\mu_i+\epsilon_{ij}yij​=μi​+ϵij​

where yijy_{ij}yij​ is the weight gain for rat jjj in diet group iii, μi\mu_iμi​ would be the mean weight gain in diet group iii and ϵij\epsilon_{ij}ϵij​ would be the deviation of rat jjj from the mean of its diet group.

Random Effects In the One-way Layout​

The simplest random effects model is the one-way layout, commonly written in the form

yij=μ+αi+ϵij,y_{ij}=\mu + \alpha_i + \epsilon_{ij},yij​=μ+αi​+ϵij​,

where j=1,…,Jj =1,\ldots,Jj=1,…,J and i=1,…,Ii =1,\ldots,Ii=1,…,I.

Normally one also assumes ϵij∼N(0,σA2)\epsilon_{ij}\sim N(0,\sigma_A^2)ϵij​∼N(0,σA2​), αi∼N(0,σA2)\alpha_i \sim N (0,\sigma_A^2)αi​∼N(0,σA2​), and that all these random variables are independent.

Note that we have stopped making a distinction in notation between random variables and measurements (the yyy -values are just random variables when distributions occur).

Details​

Note that this is considerably different from the fixed effect model.

Since the factor has changed to a random variable with an expected value of zero, the expected value of all the yyy is the same:

Eyij=μEy_{ij}=\muEyij​=μ

Vyij=σA2+σ2Vy_{ij}=\sigma^2_A + \sigma^2Vyij​=σA2​+σ2

Linear Mixed Effects Models (lmm)​

The simplest mixed effects model is

yij=μ+αi+βj+ϵijy_{ij} = \mu + \alpha_i + \beta_j + \epsilon_{ij}yij​=μ+αi​+βj​+ϵij​

where μ,α1,α2,…,αi\mu, \alpha_1, \alpha_2, \ldots, \alpha_iμ,α1​,α2​,…,αi​ are unknown constants,

βj∼N(0,σβ2)\beta_j \sim N(0,\sigma^2_\beta)βj​∼N(0,σβ2​)

ϵij∼N(0,σ2)\epsilon_{ij} \sim N(0,\sigma^2)ϵij​∼N(0,σ2)

(βj\beta_jβj​ and ϵij\epsilon_{ij}ϵij​ independent).

Details​

The μ\muμ and αi\alpha_iαi​ are the fixed effects and βj\beta_jβj​ is the random effects. Recall that in the simple one-way layout with yij=μ+αi+ϵijy_{ij} = \mu + \alpha_i + \epsilon_{ij}yij​=μ+αi​+ϵij​, we can write the model in matrix form y‾=Xβ‾+ϵ‾\underline{y} = X \underline{\beta} + \underline{\epsilon}y​=Xβ​+ϵ​ where β‾=(μ,α1,…,αI)′\underline{\beta} = (\mu, \alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_I)'β​=(μ,α1​,…,αI​)′ and XXX is appropriately chosen.

The same applies to the simplest random effects model yij=μ+βj+ϵijy_{ij}= \mu + \beta_j+ \epsilon_{ij}yij​=μ+βj​+ϵij​ where we can write y‾=μ⋅1‾+ZU‾+ϵ‾\underline{y} = \mu \cdot \underline{1}+ Z \underline{U} + \underline{\epsilon}y​=μ⋅1​+ZU​+ϵ​ where 1‾=(1,1,…,1)′\underline{1}=(1,1, \ldots, 1)'1​=(1,1,…,1)′, U‾=(β1,…,βJ)′\underline{U} = ( \beta_1, \ldots, \beta_J )'U​=(β1​,…,βJ​)′.

In general, we write the mixed effects models in matrix form with y‾=Xβ‾+ZU‾+ϵ‾\underline{y} = X \underline{\beta} + Z \underline{U} + \underline {\epsilon}y​=Xβ​+ZU​+ϵ​, where β‾\underline{\beta}β​ contains the fixed effects and U‾\underline{U}U​ contains the random effects.

Examples​

Example

  1. yi=β1+β2xi+ϵiy_i = \beta_1 + \beta_2 x_i + \epsilon_iyi​=β1​+β2​xi​+ϵi​ (SLR)

  2. yij=μ+αi+βixij+ϵijy_{ij} = \mu + \alpha_i + \beta_i x_{ij} + \epsilon_{ij}yij​=μ+αi​+βi​xij​+ϵij​ only fixed effects (ANCOVA)

  3. yijk=μ+αi+bj+ϵijky_{ijk} = \mu + \alpha_i + b_j + \epsilon_{ijk}yijk​=μ+αi​+bj​+ϵijk​ where αi\alpha_iαi​ are fixed but bjb_jbj​ are random.

  4. yijk=μ+αi+bjxij+ϵijky_{ijk} = \mu + \alpha_i + b_j x_{ij} + \epsilon_{ijk}yijk​=μ+αi​+bj​xij​+ϵijk​ where αi\alpha_iαi​ are fixed but bjb_jbj​ are random slopes.
